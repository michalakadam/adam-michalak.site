# Professional excellence

## What Aristotle can teach us about producing quality output

There are people I've worked with who seem to do the right thing every time. Not through visible effort, not through grinding - they just operate at a level where excellent conduct looks automatic.

For years I assumed they were simply more disciplined than me. That they had more willpower, or fewer bad impulses to manage. But Aristotle had a more precise way of thinking about this, and it changed how I approach my own development.

## The four states of character

Aristotle didn't just distinguish between good and bad character. He mapped a spectrum of four states, based on the alignment between what you know is right and what you actually want.

The **virtuous** person does the right thing because they want to. Their character has been shaped so that desire and reason point in the same direction. There is no internal friction.

The **continent** person knows what is right and does it - but through effort. They feel the pull of the wrong choice and override it. From the outside this looks like discipline. From the inside it feels like a fight.

The **incontinent** person also knows what is right - but loses the fight. The desire wins. They know they should focus, but they open the phone. They know the shortcut is wrong, but they take it anyway. The knowledge is there; the execution fails.

The **vicious** person is the rarest case. They don't just fail to do the right thing - they actively want the wrong thing. Their values themselves are misaligned. This isn't weakness; it's a different compass.

One critical observation: virtue is domain-specific. When someone tears apart my code in a review or challenges my architecture, I update my position without defensiveness - almost reflexively. But when it comes to public feedback on my writing - a cynical LinkedIn comment, a dismissive reply to a blog post - I am highly defensive and avoidant. Same person, same day, completely different internal response. This means that being virtuous in one domain tells you nothing about adjacent domains. Each area has its own rewiring cost, and pretending otherwise leads to blind spots.

## Load-bearing virtues

Here is where I part ways with the classical ideal: I think that striving for universal virtue is a resource sink.

Ask yourself: ["Does this build?"](https://adam-michalak.site/articles/constructive-principle)

Some habits and behaviors are decorative, while others are load-bearing infrastructure. A load-bearing virtue is a behavior that, if made effortless, unlocks compounding capabilities across the rest of your life. Physical health, deep focus, emotional regulation under pressure - these tend to be load-bearing.

Then there are behaviors that feel virtuous but are decorative: fixing minor household issues the moment you notice them instead of leaving them alone until their priority rises high enough to justify the effort. Grinding through a queue of easy, low-leverage work tickets just to feel productive. Forcing a high-friction, socially celebrated exercise habit like running when a zero-friction alternative achieves the same biological baseline. Effort spent on the appearance of optimization rather than the substance of it.

The test is simple: if this behavior became effortless tomorrow, would it make other things in my life significantly easier? If yes, it's load-bearing. If it would just feel nice, it's decorative. The grind required to achieve a state of virtue should be reserved strictly for the areas that expand your capacity to play non-linear games.

There is a subtler trap here that is worth naming, especially in the age of AI. Sometimes we do not build virtue at all - we buy better tooling to mask the leak.

I have a natural resistance to building ugly proof-of-concepts. I prefer to build systems correctly the first time. In Aristotelian terms, this is an incontinent friction: I know that rapid prototyping and micro-experimentation is the correct approach in ambiguous situations, but my appetite for clean architecture resists it. Rather than grinding through that resistance, I use AI to rapidly build the final, correct version - bypassing the entire friction of the messy experimentation phase.

This is highly efficient. It works. But it hides a structural dependency: the underlying incontinence is never resolved. If the tooling disappears, the friction returns in full. I have not built the virtue of tolerating ugliness - I have outsourced the continence to a machine. Whether that trade-off is acceptable depends on how permanent you believe the tooling to be. I have decided the efficiency gain is worth the hidden cost.

## Mapping your operating system

How do you figure out which virtues are load-bearing for you?

I actually did this exercise with AI. First I explored the Aristotelian framework in depth - virtuous, continent, incontinent, vicious - until I understood the distinctions well enough to use them honestly. Then I asked for a structured questionnaire to map my own behaviors against the framework: where am I virtuous, where am I continent, where am I incontinent, and where am I deliberately letting go?

The result was a map of my operating system. Here's what it looks like in practice.

**Where I am already there.** These are behaviors that run on autopilot - automated system architecture requiring zero willpower.

- **Biological recovery.** Hard stop on professional work at 9 PM. In bed by 10. There is no internal debate. I've learned the hard way that pushing past this window degrades everything downstream, so the boundary is automated.
- **Cognitive offloading.** Complex state is never held in the head. Messy technical problems get immediately externalized into an Obsidian vault or mapped out with AI. Mental roadblocks don't linger because offloading thought is the path of least resistance.
- **Ego-free technical judgment.** When someone presents a better argument in a code review or architecture discussion, I update my position. That used to take effort. Now it's an automatic optimization reflex, not an emotional struggle.
- **Tolerating ambiguity.** Plunging into an undocumented system or a vague problem space does not trigger anxiety. The unknown is naturally processed as a puzzle to be enjoyed and systematically dissected.
- **Structural deconstruction.** Comprehension is built by naturally seeking the "why." Digging deeper to understand why a system works or fails - beyond its surface-level syntax - is a default setting.
- **Somatic maintenance.** Physical movement is driven by physiological craving, not intellectual force. A daily 45-minute forest walk and twice-weekly bouldering sessions are non-negotiable infrastructure, fiercely protected no matter how high the cognitive load gets.

**Where I'm still continent - managing with structure, not willpower.** Sustained focus on tedious work doesn't come naturally to me. I maintain it through [environmental constraints](https://adam-michalak.site/articles/turning-effort-into-reward): the phone stays physically out of reach, notifications are locked down, only my wife can break through. The behavior looks like discipline from the outside, but it's really just environmental design doing the heavy lifting.

**Where I've deliberately let go.** Every spring I used to force myself through a cycle of running - ten, fifteen, twenty sessions before the habit collapsed. The friction was enormous and the failure predictable. I stopped trying to grind through it and switched to rucking instead - weighted walks that fit naturally into the forest walks I was already doing. Same zone 2 cardiovascular benefit, a fraction of the friction. It freed up the cognitive overhead I was wasting on failing at running.

## Managing the blast radius

You do not necessarily need to eliminate all incontinent or vicious behaviors. You need to manage their blast radius.

For weak willpower - I deliberately allow myself to pick up low-leverage, easy tasks at work purely for the immediate dopamine hit. Fixing a trivial bug, clearing a simple ticket, reorganizing a config file - none of these move the needle on my core R&D goals. But forcing 100% deep-focus efficiency requires universal continence, and universal continence leads to total system failure. Burnout. By intentionally allowing low-stakes dopamine tasks to act as a pressure release valve, I preserve cognitive reserves for the load-bearing work. It is a deliberate trade-off: I leak a controlled amount of efficiency to keep the system running at sustainable capacity.

If the incontinent behavior threatens your core goals, do not try to grind your way out of it with continence alone. Change the environment so the failure state is physically difficult to execute.

Allow yourself to be imperfect in low-stakes domains so that you have the cognitive reserves necessary to maintain continence in the areas you are actively trying to rewire into virtue. Strategic negligence of minor flaws is a feature of a robust system, not a bug.
